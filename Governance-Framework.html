In the war room, the Junior Developer was pale. He was staring at a terminal that was spewing HTTP 500 errors like a severed artery. "But the AI knew the plan," he stammered. "I pasted the requirements. It said it understood."

I didn't care about what the AI said. I care about evidence. I pushed his chair aside and opened the project folder. I wasn't looking at code anymore; I was looking at a digital crime scene.

The Telecom Veteran's Realization

That night, amidst the wreckage, my 17 years in Telecom reliability kicked in. In 2008, if I touched a live switch without a log entry, I was fired. Why was I letting a chatbot write production code without a plan?

We didn't have a coding problem. We had an Epistemic Collapse. We were treating the AI like a magic wand, when we should have been treating it like a high-risk, high-reward Junior Engineer that requires governance.
